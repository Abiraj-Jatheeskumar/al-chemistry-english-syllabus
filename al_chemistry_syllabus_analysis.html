📌 Competency Levels (1.1–1.5)
  • 1.1 Reviews models of atomic structure — cathode rays, Rutherford model, atomic number, mass number, isotopes, nuclides, relative atomic mass
  • 1.2 Investigates electromagnetic radiation — wave-particle dual nature, c=λν, E=hν, E=mc², electromagnetic spectrum
  • 1.3 Electronic energy levels — successive ionization energies, Bohr model, hydrogen atomic spectrum, four quantum numbers (n, l, ml, ms), shapes of s and p orbitals
  • 1.4 Ground state electronic configurations — Hund's rule, Pauli exclusion principle, Aufbau principle and deviations, Z=1 to 54 and their ions
  • 1.5 Periodic table and atomic properties — s, p, d, f blocks, shielding effect, effective nuclear charge, atomic radii, ionization energy, electron gain energy, electronegativity (Pauling scale)
⚛️ Core Concepts
  • Properties of cathode rays and their demonstration
  • Rutherford's nuclear model from gold leaf experiment conclusions
  • de Broglie equation: λ = h/mv — wave-particle duality
  • Electromagnetic spectrum and photon energy calculations
  • Bohr model and quantization of energy in hydrogen
  • Lyman, Balmer, Paschen series in hydrogen spectrum
  • Four quantum numbers and their physical meanings
  • Shapes of s (spherical) and p (dumbbell) orbitals
  • Hund's rule, Pauli exclusion, Aufbau principle
  • Deviation of Pd in 4d series from Aufbau (accepted config)
  • Stable configurations: s², p³, p⁶, d⁵, d¹⁰
  • Shielding effect and effective nuclear charge (qualitative)
  • Atomic radii: covalent, van der Waals, metallic
  • Zigzag variation of first ionization energy across period
  • Electronegativity — Pauling scale only
📐 Key Equations
c = λν (speed of light = wavelength × frequency)
E = hν (Planck's equation — energy of photon)
E = mc² (Einstein — mass-energy equivalence)
λ = h/mv (de Broglie equation — matter waves)
ΔE = hν (energy difference between levels = photon energy)
🔗 Prerequisites & Connections
  • Prerequisite: O/L knowledge of atoms, elements, periodic table
  • → Unit 02: Electronic configuration determines bonding behaviour
  • → Unit 05: Ionization energies used in Born-Haber cycles
  • → Unit 06: Electronic config of d-block explains variable oxidation states
  • → Unit 12: Quantum theory basis for understanding energy levels in reactions
⚠️ Common Student Mistakes
  • Confusing atomic number and mass number in isotope notation
  • Incorrectly applying Aufbau — especially for Cr and Cu (exceptions)
  • Mixing up quantum numbers (n vs l vs ml)
  • Drawing electronic configurations of ions without removing outer electrons first
  • Confusing successive ionization energy jumps with period changes
  • Not knowing the difference between covalent radius, van der Waals and metallic radius
  • Claiming ionization energy always increases across a period (not zigzag)

🎓 Student-Friendly Guide — Unit 01
What You Really Learn
How scientists discovered the atom's structure and how electrons are arranged in distinct energy levels described by quantum numbers. This unit builds your entire understanding of "why atoms behave the way they do."
Why This Matters
Every unit in A/L Chemistry builds on atomic structure. Bonding, reactivity, periodic trends, organic mechanisms — all trace back to how electrons are arranged. Master this first.
Students Usually Struggle With
Writing configurations for ions (especially d-block ions lose 4s before 3d), understanding why ionization energy zigzags (group 13 < group 2, group 16 < group 15), and visualizing orbital shapes.
Must Memorize
Electronic configurations of Z=1 to 54 (especially exceptions: Cr=[Ar]3d⁵4s¹, Cu=[Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹, Pd=[Kr]4d¹⁰). Pauling electronegativity trend. All four quantum number rules.
Must Understand Conceptually
Why electrons fill in specific order (energy minimization). Why d⁵ and d¹⁰ are stable (exchange energy / symmetry). Why shielding reduces effective nuclear charge. How Bohr model explains hydrogen spectrum lines.
Exam Focus Areas
Writing electron configs. Identifying block from config. Graph of successive ionization energies (predicting group/period). Calculating E, λ, ν using photon equations. Quantum number identification.

📌 Competency Levels (2.1–2.3)
  • 2.1 Primary interactions — covalent (single, multiple), Lewis structures, polarity, dipole moment, dative covalent bonding, ionic bonding, ionic lattice, covalent character of ionic bonds (Fajans' rules), metallic bonding
  • 2.2 Shapes of molecules — resonance structures, hybridization (sp, sp², sp³), σ and π bonds, VSEPR theory, all molecular shapes up to 6 electron pairs
  • 2.3 Secondary interactions — van der Waals forces (dipole-dipole, ion-dipole, hydrogen bonding, ion-induced dipole, dipole-induced dipole, London dispersion forces), molecular lattices (I₂ and H₂O)
🔷 Key Molecular Shapes (VSEPR)
  • Linear — 2 bond pairs (e.g. BeCl₂, CO₂)
  • Trigonal planar — 3 bp, 0 lp (e.g. BF₃, SO₃)
  • Tetrahedral — 4 bp, 0 lp (e.g. CH₄, CCl₄)
  • Pyramidal — 3 bp, 1 lp (e.g. NH₃, PCl₃)
  • Angular/Bent — 2 bp, 2 lp (e.g. H₂O, SO₂)
  • Trigonal bipyramid — 5 bp, 0 lp (e.g. PCl₅)
  • See-saw — 4 bp, 1 lp (e.g. SF₄)
  • T-shaped — 3 bp, 2 lp (e.g. ClF₃)
  • Octahedral — 6 bp, 0 lp (e.g. SF₆)
  • Square pyramid — 5 bp, 1 lp (e.g. BrF₅)
  • Square planar — 4 bp, 2 lp (e.g. XeF₄)
🔗 Hybridization Guide
  • sp hybridization — linear geometry, 2 σ bonds + 2 π bonds (e.g. alkynes, BeCl₂)
  • sp² hybridization — trigonal planar, 3 σ + 1 π (e.g. alkenes, BF₃)
  • sp³ hybridization — tetrahedral, 4 σ bonds (e.g. alkanes, NH₃, H₂O)
  • σ bond: linear (head-on) overlap of orbitals — stronger
  • π bond: lateral (sideways) overlap of p orbitals — weaker
  • Single bond = 1σ. Double bond = 1σ+1π. Triple bond = 1σ+2π
📋 Resonance Examples
  • O₃ (ozone) — equal O-O bond lengths explained by resonance
  • CO₃²⁻ (carbonate) — 3 equivalent C-O bonds, delocalized electrons
  • NO₃⁻ (nitrate) — 3 resonance structures, equal bond lengths
  • NO₂, CO₂, N₂O — simple resonance structures required
  • Max 10 atoms in resonance structures (syllabus limit)
🌊 Secondary Interactions & Effects
  • Hydrogen bonding: anomalous high bp of H₂O, HF, NH₃ vs. hydrides
  • Group 15 hydrides: NH₃ ≫ PH₃ (H-bonding in NH₃)
  • Group 16 hydrides: H₂O ≫ H₂S (H-bonding in H₂O)
  • Group 17 hydrides: HF ≫ HCl (H-bonding in HF)
  • London dispersion forces increase with molar mass → higher bp in alkane series
  • Molecular lattices: I₂ (molecular crystal, London forces) and H₂O (ice, hydrogen bonds)
⚠️ Common Student Mistakes
  • Forgetting lone pairs when predicting molecular shape
  • Confusing electron pair geometry with molecular geometry
  • Incorrectly drawing Lewis structures (especially for expanded octet)
  • Confusing σ and π bonds
  • Not applying Fajans' rules correctly (polarizing power vs. polarizability)
  • Drawing bond angles as exact values (syllabus does not require exact values)
🎓 Student-Friendly Guide — Unit 02
What You Really Learn
How atoms join together (bonding) and what 3D shapes the resulting molecules have (structure). You learn to predict shapes, polarity, and physical properties from bonding type.
Students Usually Struggle With
VSEPR — remembering all 11 shapes and when lone pairs are present. Hybridization of central atoms. Drawing correct Lewis structures for ions and expanded octet molecules.
Must Memorize
All VSEPR shapes. sp/sp²/sp³ characteristics. Resonance molecules listed in syllabus (O₃, CO₃²⁻, NO₃⁻, NO₂). Types of van der Waals forces. Hydrogen bond donors (N-H, O-H, F-H).
Must Understand Conceptually
Why lone pairs repel more than bonding pairs (affect shape/angle). Why delocalization stabilizes resonance structures. Why hydrogen bonds cause anomalously high boiling points. Why covalent character increases in ionic compounds with high charge-density cations.

📌 Competency Levels (3.1–3.3)
  • 3.1 Chemical formulae — oxidation numbers, IUPAC nomenclature, moles and Avogadro constant, empirical/molecular formulae, composition parameters (mass fraction, volume fraction, mole fraction, ppm, ppb, w/w%, v/v%, mg dm⁻³, µg dm⁻³)
  • 3.2 Balancing equations — inspection method, redox method (oxidation number method, half ionic equation method), balancing nuclear reactions, concentration (mol dm⁻³, mol m⁻³), preparation of solutions, glassware handling
  • 3.3 Stoichiometry calculations — acid-base, redox, precipitation (gravimetry) calculations, preparation of standard Na₂CO₃ solution
📐 Composition Parameters
  • Mass fraction: m(solute)/m(solution)
  • w/w%: mass fraction × 100
  • v/v%: volume of solute / total volume × 100
  • Mole fraction: n(A) / n(total)
  • ppm (mass): mg kg⁻¹
  • ppb (mass): µg kg⁻¹
  • ppm (volume/gas): µL L⁻¹
  • ppm for dilute aqueous: mg dm⁻³
  • ppb for dilute aqueous: µg dm⁻³
  • Concentration: mol dm⁻³ (molarity)
⚖️ Balancing Methods
  • Inspection method: Trial and error, balance atoms and charges
  • Oxidation number method: Identify change in oxidation states, equalize electron gain/loss
  • Half ionic equation method: Write separate oxidation and reduction half-reactions, balance electrons, combine
  • Nuclear reactions: Balance mass numbers and atomic numbers separately
🧪 Practical Experiments
🔬 Handling pipette, burette, beakers, measuring cylinders and four-beam balance
🔬 Preparation of standard solution of Na₂CO₃
💡 Oxidation Number Rules
  • Free elements: ON = 0
  • Monoatomic ions: ON = charge
  • H in compounds: +1 (–1 in metal hydrides)
  • O in compounds: –2 (–1 in peroxides, +2 in OF₂)
  • Halogens: –1 (except when bonded to more electronegative atom)
  • Sum of ON = charge of species
🎓 Student-Friendly Guide — Unit 03

Why This Matters
Every A/L exam calculation traces back to stoichiometry. Units 05, 11, 12, 13 all require this unit's skills. Exam questions almost always involve mol calculations.
Most Important Formulas
n = m/M. n = CV. Mole fraction = nA/(nA+nB+...). ppm and ppb conversions. Empirical formula from % composition. Stoichiometric ratios from balanced equations.

📌 Competency Levels (4.1–4.5)
  • 4.1 Three states of matter — arrangement of particles, macroscopic properties (volume, density, shape, compressibility)
  • 4.2 Ideal gas model — ideal gas equation and derivatives (PV=nRT, P=ρRT, PM=dRT), Boyle's law, Charles's law, Avogadro's law, molar volume
  • 4.3 Kinetic molecular theory — pressure of gas, mean/mean square/root mean square speed, kinetic molecular equation PV=⅓mNC̄², Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution (graphical)
  • 4.4 Dalton's law of partial pressures
  • 4.5 Real gases — compressibility factor, deviations from ideal behaviour, van der Waals equation (qualitative), critical temperature
📐 Key Equations
PV = nRT (Ideal gas equation)
P = CRT (C = concentration = n/V)
PM = dRT (M = molar mass, d = density)
PV = (m/M)RT
PV = ⅓mNC̄² (Kinetic molecular equation)
C̄² = 3RT/M (mean square speed)
P_total = P_A + P_B + ... (Dalton's law)
P_A = x_A × P_total (partial pressure)
📊 Graphs to Know
  • Boyle's law: PV = constant (hyperbola), P vs 1/V (straight line)
  • Charles's law: V/T = constant (straight line through origin)
  • Maxwell-Boltzmann: Speed distribution curve — effect of T and M on peak position and curve shape
  • Compressibility factor: Z = PV/nRT vs P for real vs ideal gas
🧪 Practical Experiments
🔬 Experimental determination of molar volume of oxygen gas
🔬 Determination of relative atomic mass of Mg using molar volume of hydrogen
⚠️ Key Points on Real Gases
  • At high pressure: real gas volume > ideal (intermolecular repulsions dominate)
  • At low pressure: real gas volume < ideal (intermolecular attractions dominant)
  • van der Waals equation adds pressure correction (a/V²) and volume correction (b)
  • Critical temperature: above this, gas cannot be liquefied regardless of pressure
  • Assumptions of ideal gas that fail for real gas: no intermolecular forces, zero molecular volume
🎓 Student-Friendly Guide — Unit 04
What You Really Learn
How gases behave mathematically under different conditions of pressure, volume, temperature, and amount. The ideal gas law unifies Boyle's, Charles's, and Avogadro's laws into one powerful equation.
Students Usually Struggle With
Choosing the correct form of the ideal gas equation. Understanding what C̄² = 3RT/M means physically. Interpreting Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution changes with temperature. Deriving partial pressure from mole fraction.
Visual Imagination Needed
Imagine gas molecules zooming around randomly, colliding with walls (creating pressure). Faster molecules = higher temperature. Maxwell-Boltzmann shows how speeds are distributed — never all the same speed.

📌 Competency Levels (5.1–5.4)
  • 5.1 Enthalpy concepts — extensive/intensive properties, system/surroundings/boundary, standard states, state functions, ΔH = H(final) – H(initial) in kJ and kJ mol⁻¹
  • 5.2 Enthalpy changes — Q=mcΔT, endothermic/exothermic, ΔH°f, ΔH°c, ΔH°d (bond dissociation), mean bond dissociation enthalpies, ΔH°neu, ΔH°hyd, ΔH°sol, Hess's law, enthalpy level diagrams and cycles
  • 5.3 Born-Haber cycle — lattice enthalpy, ΔH°s, ΔH°vap, ΔH°fus, ΔH°atm, ΔH°I (ionization), ΔH°EA (electron gain), ΔH°LE (lattice)
  • 5.4 Spontaneity — entropy (S) and entropy change (ΔS), Gibbs energy (G), ΔG = ΔH – TΔS, standard values ΔG°, ΔH°, ΔS°; spontaneity criterion (ΔG < 0)
📐 Key Equations
Q = mcΔT (heat change at constant pressure)
ΔH = H(products) – H(reactants)
ΔH°rxn = ΣΔH°f(products) – ΣΔH°f(reactants)
ΔH°rxn ≈ ΣΔH°d(bonds broken) – ΣΔH°d(bonds formed)
ΔG = ΔH – TΔS
ΔG° = ΔH° – TΔS°
ΔG° = ΣG°(products) – ΣG°(reactants)
ΔS = ΣS(products) – ΣS(reactants)
🏗️ Born-Haber Cycle Components
  • ΔH°f — Standard enthalpy of formation (overall)
  • ΔH°s — Enthalpy of sublimation (solid metal → gas)
  • ΔH°atm — Enthalpy of atomization (½ dissociation of X₂)
  • ΔH°I — Enthalpy of ionization (successive IEs)
  • ΔH°EA — Enthalpy of electron gain (electron affinity)
  • ΔH°LE — Lattice enthalpy (ion lattice → gaseous ions)
  • Hess's Law: ΔH°f = ΔH°s + ΔH°atm + ΣΔH°I + ΣΔHEA – ΔHLE
🧪 Practical Experiments
🔬 Experimental determination of enthalpy of neutralization (NaOH/HCl, KOH/HNO₃, NaOH/CH₃COOH, NH₄OH/HCl)
🔬 Validation of Hess's law: preparing 1 mol dm⁻³ NaCl, 250 cm³ by two methods
🎯 Spontaneity — ΔG Analysis
  • ΔG < 0: Spontaneous (feasible) reaction
  • ΔG = 0: System at equilibrium
  • ΔG > 0: Non-spontaneous (reverse is spontaneous)
  • High temperature favours reactions with +ΔS (entropy driven)
  • Low temperature favours reactions with –ΔH (enthalpy driven)
  • ΔG and ΔS are state functions (reported as kJ mol⁻¹ and J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹)
⚠️ Common Student Mistakes
  • Mixing sign conventions (exothermic ΔH < 0, endothermic ΔH > 0)
  • Forgetting to account for stoichiometric coefficients in Hess cycles
  • Confusing bond dissociation enthalpy (always positive) with bond formation enthalpy
  • Using mean bond enthalpies for lattice enthalpy calculations (wrong — use Born-Haber)
  • Not distinguishing ΔG and ΔG° in spontaneity discussions
  • Wrong units: ΔG in kJ mol⁻¹ but ΔS in J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ — must convert before TΔS
🎓 Student-Friendly Guide — Unit 05
What You Really Learn
The energy story of chemical reactions — why some release heat (exothermic), some absorb heat (endothermic), and what determines whether a reaction will actually happen (ΔG). You also learn to construct Born-Haber cycles to find lattice energies.
Most Important Formulas
Q=mcΔT. ΔG=ΔH–TΔS. ΔH°rxn from formation enthalpies. ΔH from bond enthalpies. Born-Haber cycle construction. Always watch units (J vs kJ, K not °C in TΔS).
Exam Focus Areas
Drawing and using enthalpy cycles. Born-Haber cycle problems. Predicting spontaneity using ΔG. Temperature effect on spontaneity. Neutralization enthalpy comparisons (strong/strong vs weak/strong).

📌 Competency Levels (6.1–6.6)

  • 6.3 Compound trends — solubility/thermal stability of s-block compounds down groups, acid/base/amphoteric nature of oxides across 3rd period, hydrolysis of halides/hydrides
  • 6.4 d-block properties — variable oxidation states, electronegativity, metallic properties, catalytic action, coloured compounds
  • 6.5 d-block compounds — Cr and Mn oxides (acidic/basic/amphoteric), CrO₄²⁻/Cr₂O₇²⁻/MnO₄⁻ as oxidizing agents
  • 6.6 Complex compounds — complexes of Cr, Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, Cu with H₂O and Cl⁻, IUPAC naming of mono-dentate complexes, reactions of d-block cations with NaOH and NH₃(aq)
🧪 Identification Tests (Qualitative Analysis)
  • Halides: AgNO₃/NH₃ (white→AgCl, cream→AgBr, yellow→AgI), Pb(NO₃)₂, Cl₂/CCl₄
  • SO₄²⁻: BaCl₂/dil HCl → white BaSO₄ precipitate (insoluble in acids)
  • SO₃²⁻: BaCl₂ → white precipitate (soluble in dil HCl)
  • S₂O₃²⁻: AgNO₃ → white → yellow → black
  • S²⁻: Pb(CH₃COO)₂ → black PbS
  • CO₃²⁻: dil HCl → CO₂ (turns lime water milky)
  • NO₃⁻: Browning test (dil H₂SO₄ + FeSO₄)
  • NO₂⁻: dil HCl → blue litmus, NaOH/Al → NH₃
  • NH₄⁺: NaOH → NH₃ (litmus + HCl + Nessler's reagent)
  • d-block cations: NaOH and NH₃(aq) reactions with characteristic colours and precipitate solubility
⚗️ Allotropes Summary
  • Carbon: Diamond (tetrahedral sp³, hardest, insulator), Graphite (hexagonal sp², soft/lubricant, conductor), Fullerene (C₆₀ spherical)
  • Oxygen: O₂ (dioxygen), O₃ (ozone — angular)
  • Sulphur: Rhombic sulphur (S₈, stable at RT), Monoclinic sulphur (S₈, metastable above 96°C)
🔗 d-Block Oxidation States of Mn
  • +2 (Mn²⁺) — pale pink
  • +4 (MnO₂) — dark brown/black
  • +6 (MnO₄²⁻) — dark green (manganate)
  • +7 (MnO₄⁻) — purple (permanganate)
  • Redox reactions used to show colour changes experimentally
🧪 Practical Experiments
🔬 Showing presence of N in air using Mg
🔬 Identification of halides using AgNO₃/NH₃, Pb(NO₃)₂, Cl₂/CCl₄
🔬 Standardization of thiosulphate using KIO₃ and KI
🔬 Identification of NH₃ and NH₄⁺ (litmus, HCl, Nessler's)
🔬 Comparing solubility of s-block salts in water
🔬 Comparing thermal stability of nitrates, carbonates, bicarbonates
🔬 Observing colours of d-block complex ions (Cu, Co, Ni)
🔬 Determination of [Fe²⁺] using acidified KMnO₄
🔬 Determination of [KMnO₄] using standard K₂C₂O₄
🔬 Reactions of Na and Mg with air, water, and acids
⚠️ Important: Halogen Disproportionation
  • Cl₂ + H₂O → HCl + HOCl (Cl: 0 → –1 and +1)
  • Cl₂ + NaOH(cold) → NaCl + NaOCl + H₂O
  • Cl₂ + NaOH(hot) → NaCl + NaClO₃ + H₂O
  • ClO⁻ (chlorate(I) ion) also disproportionates on heating
  • Relative oxidizing power: F₂ > Cl₂ > Br₂ > I₂
  • Displacement: stronger oxidizer displaces weaker halide ion
🎓 Student-Friendly Guide — Unit 06
What You Really Learn
The practical chemistry of real elements — how they react, what compounds they form, patterns and trends across the periodic table, and how to identify ions in the lab. This is the "doing chemistry" unit.
Why This Is Hard

Must Memorize
Flame test colours. All identification tests for anions and cations. Allotropes and their properties. All balanced equations for reactions of s-block metals. Oxidation states of N, Cl, S, Mn, Cr compounds. IUPAC names of complexes.
Exam Focus Areas
Writing balanced ionic equations for redox reactions of permanganate and dichromate. Qualitative analysis of anions and cations. Trends in solubility and thermal stability across periods and down groups. Complex ion colours and ligand identification.

  • 7.4 Isomerism — constitutional isomers (chain, position, functional group), stereoisomers (diastereomers illustrated by geometrical isomers, enantiomers for optical isomerism)
🔬 Isomerism Types
  • Constitutional (structural) isomers: Same molecular formula, different connectivity
  • Chain isomers: Different carbon chain length/branching
  • Position isomers: Same chain, functional group at different position
  • Functional group isomers: Same formula, different functional group (e.g. alcohol vs ether)
  • Geometrical isomers (cis/trans): Restricted rotation around C=C, each C must have 2 different groups
  • Optical isomers (enantiomers): Chiral centre (4 different groups), non-superimposable mirror images
📛 IUPAC Nomenclature Syllabus Limits
  • Main chain: maximum 6 carbon atoms
  • Only saturated, unbranched, unsubstituted side chains (alkyl groups only)
  • Maximum one C=C or C≡C (not a substituent — part of main chain)
  • Maximum 2 substituent groups
  • Substituents: –F, –Cl, –Br, –I, –CH₃, –C₂H₅, –OH, –NH₂, –NO₂, –CN, –CHO, >C=O
  • Principal functional groups: –OH, –CHO, >C=O, –COOH, –COX, –COOR, –NH₂, –CONH₂
  • Nomenclature of aromatic compounds NOT tested
⚠️ Common Mistakes
  • Not choosing the longest carbon chain
  • Numbering chain from wrong end (give lowest locants to principal group/substituent)
  • Confusing position and functional group isomers
  • Claiming geometric isomerism is possible when two identical groups on one carbon
  • Forgetting that IUPAC does not test aromatic nomenclature

📐 Key Equations
Rate = –(1/a)(ΔCA/Δt) = (1/d)(ΔCD/Δt)
Rate = k[A]ˣ[B]ʸ (rate law)
Zero order: Rate = k; units of k = mol dm⁻³ s⁻¹
First order: Rate = k[A]; units of k = s⁻¹
Second order: Rate = k[A]²; units of k = mol⁻¹ dm³ s⁻¹
First order half-life: t₁/₂ = ln2/k = 0.693/k (independent of initial concentration)

  • Second order: 1/[A] vs t → straight line
  • Energy profile (single step): reactants → transition state peak → products
  • Energy profile (multi-step): multiple peaks (transition states) and troughs (intermediates)
  • Catalyst effect: lower peak (Ea) on energy profile diagram

📐 Key Equations (Equilibrium)
Kc = [C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ / [A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ (equilibrium constant expression)
Kp = Kc(RT)^Δn (Δn = moles gas products – moles gas reactants)
pH = –log[H⁺] (definition)
Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C
Ka = [H⁺][A⁻]/[HA] (acid dissociation constant)
Ka × Kb = Kw (conjugate pair relationship)
Henderson: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA])
Ksp = [Mⁿ⁺][Xⁿ⁻] (for sparingly soluble MX)
Raoult's law: PA = xA × P*A
KD = [solute in solvent A] / [solute in solvent B]

📊 Titration Curves — Key Features

Advanced Knowledge Connections

Unit 01: Atomic Structure → Unit 02: Bonding

Electronic configuration directly determines bonding behaviour. The number of valence electrons determines which bonds an atom forms. Hybridization (sp, sp², sp³) derives from atomic orbital theory. Pauling electronegativity values (from atomic structure) predict bond polarity and type. The shielding effect and effective nuclear charge explain why Period 2 elements have different bonding than Period 3.

Unit 02: Bonding → Unit 05: Energetics

Bond dissociation enthalpies (ΔH°d) come directly from bond type and strength (related to σ vs π, bond order). Born-Haber cycles require understanding ionic bonding (lattice enthalpy) and covalent bonding (atomization enthalpies). The stability of compounds (ΔG°) is related to the strength and type of chemical bonds. Resonance delocalization reduces energy, making compounds more stable.

Unit 05: Energetics → Unit 11: Kinetics

Activation energy (Ea) is the central concept connecting thermodynamics to kinetics. Exothermic reactions (ΔH < 0) have lower Ea in forward direction than reverse. Energy profiles (kinetics) are built on enthalpy diagrams (thermodynamics). ΔG determines if a reaction is thermodynamically spontaneous; Ea determines how fast it occurs. These are independent — a spontaneous reaction can be slow if Ea is high.

Unit 05: Energetics → Unit 12: Equilibrium

ΔG° = –RT·lnK — standard Gibbs energy is directly related to the equilibrium constant. A large negative ΔG° means K is very large (product-favoured equilibrium). Temperature affects both ΔG (through TΔS term) and K (through Le Chatelier's principle — endothermic reactions have K increasing with T). Entropy (ΔS) is the thermodynamic basis for understanding why equilibrium favours certain states.

Unit 12: Equilibrium → Unit 13: Electrochemistry

Electrode equilibria (reversible electrodes) are examples of equilibrium systems at electrode surfaces. The equilibrium between oxidized and reduced forms (e.g. Fe³⁺/Fe²⁺) is expressed as a standard electrode potential E°. Ksp (from Unit 12.4) determines solubility of AgCl in AgCl electrode solutions. EMF of a cell relates to ΔG° of the overall cell reaction, directly linking electrochemistry to thermodynamics.

Units 07→08→09→10 → Progressive Build

Organic chemistry is a progressive story: Unit 07 provides the language (nomenclature, functional groups, isomerism). Unit 08 establishes fundamental reaction types using hydrocarbons (free radical, electrophilic addition, electrophilic substitution). Unit 09 extends these patterns to oxygen-containing compounds, adding nucleophilic addition (C=O) and substitution (acyl C). Unit 10 completes the picture with nitrogen chemistry and demonstrates how functional group changes alter properties (basicity, reactivity).

Unit 01: Periodic Trends → Unit 06: Element Chemistry

Every trend in Unit 06 traces back to electronic structure. Why Mg reacts more slowly than Na with water? Lower reactivity due to smaller Mg²⁺ formation being less energetically favorable per electron given up vs Na⁺. Why does aluminium show amphoteric behaviour? Its 3+ charge creates intermediate electronegativity between metals and nonmetals. Why do d-block elements show variable oxidation states? Because 3d and 4s electrons have similar energies and can both be used in bonding.

Units 01-13 → Unit 14: Industrial

Unit 14 is the grand synthesis of all previous learning. Haber process optimization uses Le Chatelier's principle (Unit 12) and kinetics knowledge (Unit 11). Contact process uses equilibrium and thermodynamics (Units 05, 12). Polymer chemistry applies organic reaction types (Units 08, 09). Electrolytic production of metals applies electrochemistry (Unit 13). Environmental pollution chemistry draws on acid-base equilibria, redox chemistry, and atmospheric chemistry.

Student-Friendly Easy Overview

A plain-language guide to each unit — what it actually means, why it matters for your exam, and exactly what to focus on. No jargon without explanation.

UNIT 01

Atomic Structure — The "Why" Behind Everything

✅ What you really learn

How scientists built the current model of the atom step by step. You learn to describe electrons using quantum numbers, write electron configurations for any element (Z=1–54), and predict periodic trends from those configurations.

🎯 Most important exam areas

• Write electron config for Z=1–54 and common ions
• Interpret graphs of successive ionization energies to identify group/period
• Calculate wavelength, frequency, energy of photons
• Identify s/p/d/f block from electron configuration
• Explain periodic trends in atomic radius, IE, EN

📌 Must memorize

Electronic configurations for Z=1–54. Exception configs: Cr [Ar]3d⁵4s¹, Cu [Ar]3d¹⁰4s¹, Pd [Kr]4d¹⁰. Quantum number ranges (n, l=0 to n-1, ml=–l to +l, ms=±½). Orbital shapes: s=sphere, p=dumbbell.

⚠️ Where students lose marks

Writing ion configs — Fe³⁺ loses 4s electrons before 3d. Forgetting the zigzag pattern in IE (Al < Mg, S < P). Mixing up mass number (protons+neutrons) with atomic number (protons only). Confusing de Broglie equation with Planck's equation.

UNIT 02

Bonding and Structure — How Atoms Stick Together

✅ What you really learn

Three ways atoms bond: by sharing electrons (covalent), giving/taking electrons (ionic), or sharing in a sea (metallic). You then predict the 3D shape of any molecule using VSEPR, and understand how intermolecular forces govern physical properties like boiling point and solubility.

🎯 Most important exam areas

• Draw correct Lewis structures for molecules and polyatomic ions
• Predict shape using VSEPR (all 11 shapes)
• Identify hybridization of central atom
• Explain hydrogen bonding anomalies (high bp of H₂O, HF, NH₃)
• Distinguish σ and π bonds
• Explain resonance in O₃, CO₃²⁻, NO₃⁻

🧠 Visual imagination guide

Think of electron pairs (bonding AND lone pairs) as balloons tied together — they repel each other to get as far apart as possible. That arrangement gives you the electron pair geometry. Remove the lone pairs visually and you see the molecular shape.

⚠️ Where students lose marks

Forgetting lone pairs change shape (NH₃ is pyramidal, not tetrahedral in molecular shape). Confusing Fajans' rules direction. Not knowing that higher-period halogens can exceed octet. Bond angles — syllabus says "exact values will NOT be tested" so don't stress them.

UNIT 05

Energetics — Will the Reaction Happen? And How Much Energy?

✅ What you really learn

The energy accounting of reactions. Enthalpy (ΔH) tells you heat released or absorbed. Hess's Law lets you calculate ΔH for reactions you can't measure directly. Born-Haber cycles reveal lattice enthalpies of ionic compounds. Gibbs energy (ΔG = ΔH – TΔS) is the master equation for predicting if a reaction will spontaneously occur.

🔢 Critical calculation steps

Born-Haber: ΔH°f = ΔH°s + ΔH°atm + ΣΔH°IE + ΣΔHEA – ΔHLE (apply correct signs!). Hess's Law: reverse an equation → change sign of ΔH, multiply equation → multiply ΔH. TΔS in ΔG: convert ΔS from J K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ to kJ K⁻¹ mol⁻¹ before using.

🧠 Conceptual understanding needed

Entropy (S) is disorder/randomness. Reactions that increase disorder (solid→gas, dissolving salts, increase in moles) have +ΔS. A spontaneous reaction minimizes Gibbs energy — it finds the best balance between wanting to release heat (–ΔH) and wanting to increase disorder (+ΔS).

⚠️ Where students lose marks

Using J and kJ without converting in ΔG = ΔH – TΔS. Sign errors in Born-Haber (electron gain enthalpy can be negative). Forgetting state symbols in enthalpy definitions (ΔHf is for formation from elements in their standard states). Mean bond enthalpies give approximate answers — not used for ionic compounds.

UNIT 11

Chemical Kinetics — How Fast? And Why?

✅ What you really learn

Kinetics is about reaction speed. You learn to write a rate law (Rate = k[A]ˣ[B]ʸ) from experiment data, determine reaction order, calculate the rate constant k, and understand what happens at the molecular level when reactions occur. You also learn how multi-step reaction mechanisms determine overall reaction rate.

🔢 Key calculation patterns

Initial rate method: compare two experiments where only [A] changes → calculate order w.r.t. A. Order from graph: if [A] vs t is linear → zero order; if ln[A] vs t is linear → first order; if 1/[A] vs t is linear → second order. Half-life constant = first order. Units of k: derived from rate = k × [conc]^(overall order).

🧠 Mechanism insight

A reaction mechanism is the step-by-step story. The slowest step (highest activation energy hump on energy profile) is the rate-determining step — it controls the overall rate. Intermediates appear as troughs between peaks on energy profiles. Transition states are at the peaks. A catalyst provides an alternative pathway with lower Ea peaks.

⚠️ Where students lose marks

Deriving wrong units for k (most common mistake). Confusing intermediates (real species in a trough) vs transition states (peak — cannot be isolated). Claiming temperature changes k for first order reactions (temperature always changes k for ALL reactions). Forgetting that half-life independence from concentration is ONLY for first order.

UNIT 12

Equilibrium — The Balance Point of Chemistry

✅ What you really learn

Equilibrium is when the forward and reverse rates of a reversible reaction are equal — not that concentrations are equal, but that they've stopped changing. This principle applies to gas reactions (Kc, Kp), acid-base chemistry (Ka, Kb, pH), buffer solutions, sparingly soluble salts (Ksp), and liquid-liquid distribution (KD).

🎯 pH Calculation Roadmap

Strong acid: pH = –log[HA]. Strong base: pOH = –log[OH⁻], pH = 14 – pOH. Weak acid: [H⁺] = √(Ka × C). Weak base: [OH⁻] = √(Kb × C). Salt of weak acid/strong base: hydrolyze anion → pH > 7. Salt of strong acid/weak base: hydrolyze cation → pH < 7. Buffer: pH = pKa + log([A⁻]/[HA]).

🧠 Le Chatelier's Principle — Think Simply

The system "fights back" against any change you make. Add more reactant → shifts right (makes more products). Increase pressure on a gas system → shifts toward fewer moles of gas. Increase temperature → shifts in endothermic direction (K changes; catalyst does NOT change K). Remove a product → shifts right.

⚠️ Where students lose marks

Including pure solids or pure liquids in Kc/Kp expressions. Not squaring/cubing concentrations in Ksp for compounds with stoichiometry >1 (e.g. Ksp of Ca₃(PO₄)₂ = [Ca²⁺]³[PO₄³⁻]²). Using wrong indicator for a titration (depends on equivalence point pH matching pKIn). Henderson equation only valid when buffer ratio is between 0.1 and 10 approximately.

UNITS 07–10

Organic Chemistry Master Pattern Guide

Organic chemistry follows predictable patterns. Once you understand these patterns, you can deduce products of reactions you've never seen before. The key is to understand what type of reaction site is present and what type of reagent is attacking.

Reaction Type 1: Free Radical Substitution

Occurs in: Alkanes (C-H bonds). Reagent: Cl₂ or Br₂ in UV light. Mechanism: homolytic cleavage → radicals → chain reaction (initiation, propagation, termination). No ionic intermediates. Product: monosubstituted halide (or mixture).

Reaction Type 2: Electrophilic Addition

Occurs in: Alkenes and alkynes (C=C or C≡C — electron-rich). Electrophiles: H⁺, Br₂, H₂SO₄, Hg²⁺. Mechanism: electrophile attacks π electrons → carbocation → nucleophile attacks. Markovnikov's rule: electrophile adds to carbon with more H (forms more stable carbocation).

Reaction Type 3: Electrophilic Substitution

Occurs in: Benzene ring (aromatic). Electrophiles: NO₂⁺ (nitration), R⁺ (Friedel-Crafts alkylation), RCO⁺ (acylation), Cl⁺/Br⁺ (halogenation with Lewis acid catalyst). Mechanism: electrophile attacks ring → arenium ion → H⁺ lost to restore aromaticity.

Reaction Type 4: Nucleophilic Substitution

Occurs in: Alkyl halides (polar C-X bond) and carboxylic acid derivatives (polar C=O of acyl group). Nucleophiles attack the positive carbon. SN2 (one step, inversion) for primary halides; SN1 (two step, via carbocation) for tertiary halides.

Reaction Type 5: Nucleophilic Addition

Occurs in: Aldehydes and ketones (C=O group). Nucleophiles: HCN (→ cyanohydrin), Grignard reagent (→ alcohol), NaBH₄/LiAlH₄ (→ alcohol), 2,4-DNP (→ hydrazone + dehydration). C=O is different from C=C — more polarized, so nucleophilic attack (not electrophilic).

Reaction Type 6: Elimination

Occurs in: Alcohols (dehydration using conc H₂SO₄ or Al₂O₃ → alkene). Competes with nucleophilic substitution in alkyl halides when base is present. Product: alkene (less substituted for E2 conditions). Competing reaction to SN1/SN2 when heated or bulky base used.

Industrial Process Key Equations — Unit 14

Haber Process — Ammonia
N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = −92 kJ mol⁻¹

Conditions: Fe catalyst, ~450°C (compromise — fast enough but not too unfavourable for K), ~150–200 atm (high pressure favours product — fewer moles gas on right), unreacted gases recycled. Le Chatelier: lower T favours products (exothermic) but too slow; higher P favours products but expensive.

Ostwald Process — Nitric Acid
4NH₃ + 5O₂ → 4NO + 6H₂O (Pt-Rh catalyst, 900°C)
2NO + O₂ → 2NO₂
3NO₂ + H₂O → 2HNO₃ + NO

NO is recycled. Final product is ~60% HNO₃. Concentrated HNO₃ obtained by distillation.

Contact Process — Sulphuric Acid
S + O₂ → SO₂ (roasting sulphur/sulphide ores)
2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃ (V₂O₅ catalyst, 450°C, ~2 atm)
SO₃ + H₂SO₄ → H₂S₂O₇ (oleum)
H₂S₂O₇ + H₂O → 2H₂SO₄

SO₃ not dissolved directly in water (forms acid mist). Oleum route used for safety and efficiency.

Solvay Process — Sodium Carbonate
NaCl + NH₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → NaHCO₃↓ + NH₄Cl
2NaHCO₃ → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O + CO₂ (heating)
